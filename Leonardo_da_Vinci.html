Mona Lisa

The Mona Lisa, painted by Leonardo da Vinci between 1503 and 1506 (with possible later refinements), is widely regarded as one of the most famous and studied works of art in history. This oil painting on a poplar wood panel portrays a seated woman, traditionally identified as Lisa Gherardini, the wife of Florentine merchant Francesco del Giocondo. The subject’s enigmatic expression—often described as a subtle smile—has fascinated viewers and scholars for centuries.

Leonardo’s mastery of sfumato, a technique that blends tones and colors without harsh lines, creates a soft, almost dreamlike transition between light and shadow, giving the figure lifelike depth. Her gaze appears to follow the viewer from any angle, enhancing the painting’s mysterious aura. The background features winding paths, a distant bridge, and an imaginary landscape, blending realism with artistic invention.

Measuring just 77 cm × 53 cm, the Mona Lisa is relatively small, yet it holds immense cultural and historical significance. Today, it is housed in the Louvre Museum in Paris, where it is protected behind bulletproof glass and admired by millions each year.
